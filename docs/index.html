Teologia da Libertação — Cronologia

Chronology of Liberation Theology (1960s–present)

An open, source-referenced chronology of Teologia da Libertação (Liberation Theology) — the Latin American theological movement that emerged from the Second Vatican Council and the 1968 Medellín conference — its key figures, its confrontation with the Vatican, and its later reception.

Last updated: 2026-07-20

About

This is a work-in-progress dataset compiled from public sources (see references). Liberation Theology is a politically and theologically contested subject: contested claims are attributed to their authors, never asserted in the site's own voice, and dates not yet verified against a primary source are flagged with a ? mark. Corrections against primary sources are welcome.

What it is
A family of Latin American theologies interpreting Christian faith from the experience of the poor and oppressed — a current of thought, not an organization with members or a headquarters.[1] [4]
Emerged
1968–1971 — from the reception of the Second Vatican Council at the 1968 Medellín conference (CELAM II) to Gustavo Gutiérrez's foundational book (Lima, 1971).[1] [4]
Foundational text
Gustavo Gutiérrez, 'Teología de la liberación. Perspectivas' (Lima, 1971; English: 'A Theology of Liberation', Orbis, 1973).[6] [5]
Core theme
The 'preferential option for the poor', consolidated by the CELAM conferences of Medellín (1968) and Puebla (1979).[1] [25]
Vatican response
Two CDF instructions signed under Cardinal Ratzinger: Libertatis Nuntius (1984), criticizing borrowings from Marxist analysis, and Libertatis Conscientia (1986), developing a positive theology of freedom and liberation.[18] [19]
Strongest base
Brazil — through the CEBs (comunidades eclesiais de base) and bishops such as Hélder Câmara and Pedro Casaldáliga; estimates of CEB numbers at the 1980s peak vary widely between sources. ?[3] [1]

Chronology

Key events in chronological order. A ? flag marks dates not yet verified against a primary source.

YearDatePlaceEvent
1950s
1955 1955-11-02 Rio de Janeiro, Brazil CELAM founded — At the First General Conference of the Latin American Episcopate (Rio de Janeiro, 25 July–4 August 1955), convoked by Pius XII through the apostolic letter 'Ad Ecclesiam Christi', the bishops resolved to create the Latin American Episcopal Council; Pius XII approved its canonical constitution on 2 November 1955, with headquarters in Bogotá.[104] [101]
1960s
1961 1961-07 Huampaní, Peru ISAL founded — Iglesia y Sociedad en América Latina (ISAL) — the radicalizing Protestant 'church and society' network (Rubem Alves, Richard Shaull, José Míguez Bonino) that was a precursor stream to liberation theology — grew out of the First Latin American Consultation on Church and Society, held at Huampaní, Peru, in July 1961 and backed by the World Council of Churches; the consultation created the Junta Latinoamericana de Iglesia y Sociedad. Scholarship places the founding consultation at Huampaní; the occasional 'Huancayo' attribution is unsupported.[28] [103]
1962 1962-10-11 Rome Second Vatican Council opens — The Council (1962–1965) opens the space that Latin American bishops later 'receive' at Medellín.[1] [4]
1965 1965-06-27 Bogotá, Colombia Camilo Torres laicized; leads the Frente Unido — Having co-founded Latin America's first sociology faculty at the Universidad Nacional after studying at Louvain, Torres asked to be reduced to lay status amid conflict with Cardinal Concha and gave his final Mass at the Church of San Diego, devoting himself to the Frente Unido popular-front movement whose weekly newspaper had launched that August. Months later he joined the ELN guerrilla.[97] [11]
1965 1965-11-16 Catacombs of Domitilla, Rome Pact of the Catacombs — Three weeks before Vatican II closed, some 40 bishops celebrated Mass in the Catacombs of Domitilla and signed the 'Pact of the Catacombs', pledging to live simply and make a 'church of the poor' central to their ministry. Hélder Câmara was a leading promoter; over 500 bishops later added their signatures. The pact became closely associated with the emerging current.[82] [50]
1966 1966-02-15 Patio Cemento, Colombia Death of Camilo Torres — The Colombian priest and sociologist, who had joined the ELN guerrilla, is killed in his first combat; later invoked as a precursor symbol, though his choice of armed struggle was not representative of liberation theologians.[11]
1967 1967-03-26 Rome Populorum Progressio — Paul VI's encyclical on 'the development of peoples', a key reference for the movement.[17]
1968 1968-07-21/25 Chimbote, Peru Gutiérrez's Chimbote lecture — Asked to speak on a 'theology of development', Gustavo Gutiérrez reframes the topic as 'Hacia una teología de la liberación'.[27] [6]
1968 1968-08-24/09-06 Bogotá / Medellín, Colombia CELAM II (Medellín) — Paul VI opens the conference in Bogotá on 24 August (first papal visit to Latin America); working sessions in Medellín 26 August – 6 September adopt the language of 'institutionalized violence' and the option for the poor.[14]
1969 1969 Princeton, United States Rubem Alves, 'A Theology of Human Hope' — Alves's Princeton dissertation, defended in 1968 as 'Towards a Theology of Liberation', was published in 1969 as 'A Theology of Human Hope'; his publisher required the retitling for the US market. Harvey Cox later observed that but for the change, Alves rather than Gutiérrez might be remembered as the 'father of liberation theology'.[86] [72] [8]
1970s
1971 1971 Lima, Peru Gutiérrez publishes 'Teología de la liberación' — The movement's foundational text; English translation 'A Theology of Liberation' follows in 1973 (Orbis).[6] [5]
1971 1971-10-23 São Félix do Araguaia, Brazil Casaldáliga becomes bishop — Paul VI named Pedro Casaldáliga prelate of São Félix and titular bishop of Altava on 27 August 1971; he received episcopal ordination on 23 October 1971 (principal consecrator Fernando Gomes dos Santos, Archbishop of Goiânia), beginning his emblematic 'church of the poor' prelature in the Araguaia (served 1971–2005).[106] [70] [56]
1972 1972-04-23/30 Santiago, Chile Christians for Socialism encounter — First Latin American Encounter of Cristianos por el Socialismo (~400 delegates); the movement had begun in April 1971 when some 80 Chilean priests declared support for socialism.[29] [16]
1972 1972-11-22 Sucre, Bolivia López Trujillo elected CELAM secretary-general — Alfonso López Trujillo was elected secretary-general of CELAM at Sucre, Bolivia, on 22 November 1972; from this post (and the CELAM presidency from 1979) he became, in the widely held reading, the leading intra-Latin-American institutional opponent of liberation theology.[101] [1]
1973 1973 Salamanca, Spain Assmann's 'Teología desde la praxis de la liberación' — Hugo Assmann published (Ediciones Sígueme, Salamanca) 'Teología desde la praxis de la liberación', a reworking of earlier texts that became one of the founding and most radical methodological statements of the current, proposing the liberating praxis of the poor as the starting point of theology.[73] [95] [1]
1973 1973-10-16 Chile Chilean bishops ban Christians for Socialism — The Chilean episcopate's April 1973 plenary condemned clergy participation in Cristianos por el Socialismo; the resulting declaration 'Fe cristiana y actuación política', signed by Cardinal Raúl Silva Henríquez, was delayed by the 11 September coup and published on 16 October 1973, barring priests and religious from the movement. The substantive decision thus predates the coup, even though the formal text appeared after it.[16] [102]
1975 1975-01 ? Vitória, Brazil First Interecclesial Meeting of the CEBs — The first national encounter of comunidades eclesiais de base gathered about 70 delegates from a dozen states, growing out of a January 1974 conversation involving Frei Betto, auxiliary bishop Luiz Gonzaga Fernandes and historian Eduardo Hoornaert. Betto became a leading organizer of these periodic meetings and a widely read popularizer of the CEBs. (Movement records; exact dates not archival.)[71] [9]
1977 1977-03-12 Aguilares, El Salvador Murder of Rutilio Grande — Jesuit priest Rutilio Grande, an advocate of peasant organizing and a friend of the newly appointed Archbishop Romero, was ambushed and killed with two companions. Many biographers (following Brockman) treat the killing as the catalyst of Romero's public turn; others (Morozzo della Rocca) argue his stance evolved from his pastoral formation. Both readings are attributed.[58] [76] [52] [64]
1977 1977-10 Solentiname, Nicaragua Somoza's National Guard destroys the Solentiname community — After Solentiname members joined the FSLN assault on the San Carlos barracks (13 October 1977), Somoza's National Guard razed Cardenal's contemplative art-and-Gospel community in retaliation and he went into exile in Costa Rica. The community's collective Gospel readings had been published as 'El Evangelio en Solentiname', a landmark text of the current.[92] [10] [51]
1979 1979-01-27/02-13 Puebla, Mexico CELAM III (Puebla) — John Paul II's opening address (28 January) warns against 're-readings' of Christ as political revolutionary while affirming the defense of human dignity; the conference document consolidates the 'preferential option for the poor'.[22] [25]
1980s
1980 1980-03-24 San Salvador Assassination of Óscar Romero — A day after a homily ordering soldiers, 'in the name of God', to cease the repression, Romero was shot dead while celebrating Mass. The 1993 UN Truth Commission for El Salvador found that former Major Roberto D'Aubuisson had given the order to kill the archbishop; the finding is attributed to that report.[65] [93] [96] [12]
1983 1983-03-04 Managua, Nicaragua John Paul II reprimands Ernesto Cardenal — As John Paul II arrived in Nicaragua on 4 March 1983, Culture Minister Cardenal knelt to greet him; the pope wagged his finger and admonished him to 'regularize' his situation with the Church — an image emblematic of the Vatican's clash with priests in the Sandinista government.[74] [10]
1984 1984 Rome / Nicaragua Cardenal suspended a divinis — Ernesto Cardenal is suspended from priestly ministry under John Paul II for holding government office.[36]
1984 1984-08-06 Rome CDF Instruction 'Libertatis Nuntius' — The Instruction on Certain Aspects of the 'Theology of Liberation', signed by Cardinal Joseph Ratzinger, warns against the uncritical borrowing of Marxist analysis while not condemning the theme of liberation itself.[18]
1984 1984-09-07 Rome, Vatican Boff's colloquy with the CDF over 'Church: Charism and Power' — Leonardo Boff was summoned to a colloquy at the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith, meeting for about two hours with its prefect, Cardinal Joseph Ratzinger, over his 1981 book 'Church: Charism and Power'. Brazilian cardinals Aloísio Lorscheider and Paulo Evaristo Arns attended in support; a Notification and a period of imposed silence followed in 1985.[55] [20]
1985 1985 ? Minneapolis / London Segundo's 'Theology and the Church' answers Cardinal Ratzinger — Juan Luis Segundo published 'Theology and the Church: A Response to Cardinal Ratzinger and a Warning to the Whole Church', a book-length rebuttal to the CDF's 1984 Instruction 'Libertatis Nuntius', arguing the critique rested on a dualistic theology at odds with Vatican II and missed what liberation theologians actually taught. It became a key primary document of the reception debate (reviewed by Avery Dulles, 1986).[53] [100] [18]
1985 1985-03-11 Rome Notification on Leonardo Boff — The CDF issues a Notification on Boff's 'Church: Charism and Power'; Boff is subsequently subjected to a period of imposed silence, lifted in 1986.[20] [7]
1985 1985-05 ? Havana, Cuba 'Fidel e a Religião' (Fidel and Religion) — Frei Betto's long interviews with Fidel Castro, conducted in May 1985, were published as 'Fidel y la religión' (Havana, 1985; English 'Fidel and Religion', 1987). Some observers credit the book with helping open a dialogue between the Cuban revolution and religious believers; that assessment is contested and attributed, not asserted.[98] [9]
1986 1986-03-22 Rome CDF Instruction 'Libertatis Conscientia' — The Instruction on Christian Freedom and Liberation gives a more positive treatment of liberation themes.[19]
1986 1986-04-09 Rome → Brazil John Paul II's letter to the Brazilian bishops — In a letter dated 9 April 1986 to the Brazilian Episcopal Conference (CNBB), John Paul II writes that — developed in fidelity to Church doctrine and the two recent CDF Instructions — 'the theology of liberation is not only opportune, but useful and necessary' ('la teologia della liberazione è non solo opportuna, ma utile e necessaria'), a passage cited by the movement's defenders.[107]
1989 1989-11-16 UCA, San Salvador UCA massacre — A Salvadoran army unit murders six Jesuits — including rector and philosopher Ignacio Ellacuría — and Elba and Celina Ramos at the Central American University.[31]
1990s
1992 1992-06-28 Petrópolis, Brazil Leonardo Boff leaves the priesthood and the Franciscans — In a letter dated 28 June 1992, Boff announced he was leaving priestly ministry and the Franciscan Order after renewed conflict with Vatican doctrinal authorities, writing that he was 'leaving the priestly ministry but not the church'.[59] [32]
1992 1992-10-12/28 Santo Domingo, Dominican Republic CELAM IV (Santo Domingo) — Held under strong Roman oversight; widely read by commentators as marginalizing liberationist currents — a characterization attributed to those commentators.[30]
1999 1999-08-27 Recife, Brazil Death of Hélder Câmara — Hélder Câmara died on 27 August 1999, aged 90. As Archbishop of Olinda e Recife he had been an internationally known advocate for the poor and for human rights under the dictatorship — subjected to press censorship (1968–1977) and repeatedly nominated for the Nobel Peace Prize — and is widely regarded as a pastoral precursor of the current.[79] [1]
2000s
2001 2001 Lima, Peru Gutiérrez enters the Dominican Order — Gustavo Gutiérrez, a diocesan priest since his 1959 ordination in Lima, is received into the Order of Preachers (Dominicans); in the same period he takes up the John Cardinal O'Hara Professorship of Theology at the University of Notre Dame. (Some accounts give 1998–1999 for the entry; Notre Dame's own memorial states 2001.)[45] [6]
2007 2006-11-26 Rome Notification on Jon Sobrino — The CDF Notification on Sobrino's christology books (dated 26 November 2006, published March 2007) finds 'erroneous or dangerous propositions' but imposes no formal silencing.[21]
2007 2007-03-11 San Salvador Archbishop bars Sobrino after the CDF Notification — After the March 2007 publication of the CDF Notification, Archbishop Fernando Sáenz Lacalle told reporters that Sobrino could not teach theology in the archdiocese unless he revised his positions. Per NCR reporting, Sobrino declined to sign or adhere, calling the procedures 'not always honest or very evangelical'; the Jesuit order said it saw no grounds for internal sanctions. (Accounts attributed to the reporting.)[90] [66] [21]
2007 2007-05-13/31 Aparecida, Brazil CELAM V (Aparecida) — Opened by Benedict XVI; Cardinal Jorge Bergoglio chairs the final-document drafting committee — Aparecida is later described by commentators as the matrix of his pontificate.[15] [37] [23]
2007 2007-09 Brazil Clodovis Boff's REB essay: 'the poor in the place of Christ' — Clodovis Boff publishes 'Teologia da Libertação e volta ao fundamento' (Revista Eclesiástica Brasileira 67/268), arguing that liberation theology 'started off on the right track but put the poor in the place of Christ', making the poor rather than Christ theology's first operative principle. He points to the 2007 Aparecida conference as a path back. (His position, attributed.)[94] [83]
2008 2008-02-22 Piracicaba, Brazil Death of Hugo Assmann — Hugo Assmann died on 22 February 2008, aged 74. He had taught for over two decades at the Methodist University of Piracicaba (UNIMEP), where in his later years he turned to educational theory (in part with Jung Mo Sung) and to critiques of the 'idolatry of the market'. The full death date, previously unknown in this dataset, is confirmed by multiple obituaries.[77] [73] [85] [1]
2008 2008-04-19 Rome, Italy Death of Alfonso López Trujillo — Cardinal Alfonso López Trujillo — CELAM secretary-general (from 1972) and president (from 1979), later president of the Pontifical Council for the Family — died in Rome on 19 April 2008; his funeral Mass was celebrated by Benedict XVI in St Peter's Basilica on 23 April.[101] [105]
2008 2008-07 Brazil Leonardo Boff's public reply to his brother — Leonardo Boff answers in REB, calling his brother's text a cause of 'perplexity and disturbance', warning it would be used to finish off liberation theology, and holding that 'to encounter the poor is to encounter Christ'. The exchange is the well-documented fraternal dispute over the current's method; both positions are attributed to their authors.[83] [94]
2010s
2013 2013-03-13 Rome Election of Pope Francis — Jorge Bergoglio becomes the first Latin American pope.[1]
2013 2013-09 Vatican Francis receives Gutiérrez — Francis meets privately with Gustavo Gutiérrez, brought by CDF prefect Gerhard Müller, Gutiérrez's friend and co-author — widely read as a rehabilitation-adjacent gesture, a reading attributed to commentators.[35]
2014 2014-07-19 Campinas, Brazil Death of Rubem Alves — Rubem Alves died on 19 July 2014 in Campinas, aged 80. Remembered by the World Council of Churches as a foundational thinker of Latin American liberation theology, he had participated in ISAL and been sought by the dictatorship; his later output spanned educational theory, chronicles, children's books and psychotherapy.[68] [99] [28]
2015 2015 New York (Orbis Books) 'On the Side of the Poor': Gutiérrez and Cardinal Müller — Orbis Books publishes 'On the Side of the Poor: The Theology of Liberation', pairing essays by Gutiérrez with those of Cardinal Gerhard Ludwig Müller — Prefect of the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith (2012–2017) and a former student of Gutiérrez who had spent summers working in Peru (the material first appeared in German as 'An der Seite der Armen', 2004). Müller praised Gutiérrez as 'one of the great theologians of our time'; commentators read the collaboration as a public marker of a thaw between Rome and liberation theology — characterizations attributed to their authors.[46] [47]
2015 2015-02-03 Vatican Romero recognized as a martyr — Pope Francis authorized a decree recognizing that Romero was killed 'in hatred of the faith' (in odium fidei), declaring him a martyr and clearing the way for beatification without a certified miracle, after years of debate over whether he had died for the faith or for political stances.[67] [93]
2015 2015-05-23 San Salvador Romero beatified — Óscar Romero is declared a martyr (February 2015) and beatified.[12]
2018 2018-05-28 Vatican Francis's letter for Gutiérrez's 90th birthday — In a letter dated 28 May 2018, ahead of Gutiérrez's 90th birthday (8 June), Pope Francis thanks him for his 'theological service' and his 'preferential love for the poor and the discarded of society', encouraging him to continue his prayer and service. Together with their 2013 meeting, the letter is widely cited as evidence of warming relations under Francis; whether it amounts to a rehabilitation of liberation theology is contested (see disambiguation).[48] [49]
2018 2018-10-14 Rome Romero canonized — Francis canonizes Óscar Romero.[12]
2019 2019-02 Managua / Rome Cardenal's suspension lifted — Francis grants Ernesto Cardenal 'absolution from all canonical censures', ending the 1984 suspension.[36]
2020s
2020 2020-03-01 Managua, Nicaragua Death of Ernesto Cardenal — The priest-poet of Solentiname dies at 95.[10]
2020 2020-08-08 Batatais, Brazil Death of Pedro Casaldáliga — Dom Pedro Casaldáliga died on 8 August 2020, aged 92, at the Claretian centre in Batatais, São Paulo; his body was taken to São Félix do Araguaia for burial. The death date, previously unverified in this dataset, is confirmed by Vatican News, the CNBB and Brazilian press.[56] [70] [87]
2020 2020-09-11 Madrid, Spain Spanish court convicts ex-colonel Montano for the UCA massacre — Spain's National Court convicted former Salvadoran colonel Inocente Orlando Montano on five counts of murder 'of a terrorist nature' for his role in the 1989 killing of the Jesuits and two women, sentencing him to 133 years. Prosecuted under universal jurisdiction because five victims held Spanish citizenship, he had been extradited from the United States in 2017 — the first criminal conviction for the massacre.[69] [78]
2021 2021-11-21/28 Mexico City (and online) First Ecclesial Assembly of Latin America — CELAM convenes bishops together with laity (over 1,000 participants), presented as being in continuity with Aparecida.[26]
2024 2024-10-22 Lima, Peru Death of Gustavo Gutiérrez — The 'father of liberation theology' dies at 96 in the Dominican convent in Lima.[33] [34]
2025 2025-05-08 Rome Election of Leo XIV — After the death of Francis (21 April 2025), Robert Prevost — for decades a bishop and missionary in Peru — is elected; liberation theologians' expressions of hope, and debate about his stance, are attributed to the sources reporting them.[42]
2025 2025-07 Brazil Clodovis Boff's open letter to CELAM — The former liberation-theology systematizer, long an internal critic, publicly criticizes what he calls an 'excess' of social emphasis by the Latin American bishops, sparking controversy.[39]
2025 2025-10-04 Rome Leo XIV's 'Dilexi te' — The first apostolic exhortation of Leo XIV, on love for the poor, begun under Francis — read by some commentators as continuity with liberationist emphases, a reading others dispute; both attributed.[24] [42]
2026 2026-01-23 Colombia Camilo Torres's remains identified — The ELN reports finding and hands over the remains of the guerrilla priest, identified some 60 years after his 1966 death.[44]
2026 2026-02-18 Bogotá, Colombia Camilo Torres's remains handed over for reburial — Sixty years after his death, and after the state missing-persons unit (UBPD) confirmed the identification by DNA, Torres's remains were handed to activist priest Javier Giraldo following a Mass at the Universidad Nacional, where President Gustavo Petro backed reburial on the campus Torres had served as chaplain.[84] [61] [44]

Gutiérrez's home: the 1968 Chimbote lecture and the 1971 foundational book (Lima); decades later, the country where Leo XIV served as bishop.[27] [6] [42]

The movement's largest base: the CEBs, the Boff brothers, Frei Betto, Hélder Câmara, Casaldáliga, Rubem Alves; host of Aparecida 2007.[3] [7] [15]

El Salvador

Óscar Romero (assassinated 1980, canonized 2018), the UCA Jesuits — Ellacuría, Sobrino — and the 1989 massacre.[12] [31]

Nicaragua

Ernesto Cardenal, Solentiname, and the Sandinista-era confrontation with John Paul II (1983 reprimand, 1984 suspension, 2019 restoration).[10] [36]

Colombia

Medellín 1968 (CELAM II), CELAM's Bogotá headquarters, precursor symbol Camilo Torres — and opponent Cardinal López Trujillo.[14] [11]

Christians for Socialism (1971–72) and its banning after the 1973 coup.[29] [16]

Uruguay

Juan Luis Segundo, author of 'The Liberation of Theology' (1975).[13]

Mexico

Host of Puebla 1979 (CELAM III) and of the First Ecclesial Assembly (2021).[22] [26]

Host of Santo Domingo 1992 (CELAM IV), widely read by commentators as marginalizing liberationist currents.[30]

Key figures

Gustavo Gutiérrez

1928–2024 · Peru

Peruvian priest (b. Lima, 8 June 1928; ordained 1959), who entered the Dominican Order (Order of Preachers) in 2001 and taught at the University of Notre Dame as John Cardinal O'Hara Professor of Theology; 'father of liberation theology' and author of the movement's foundational 1971 book. Never formally condemned by the Vatican — a point he himself insisted on — and in later years publicly esteemed by Cardinal Gerhard Müller (his former student and a CDF prefect) and by Pope Francis.[6] [33] [45] [38] [47]

Leonardo Boff

b. 1938 · Brazil

Leonardo Boff (b. 1938, Concórdia, Brazil) is a Brazilian theologian and former Franciscan priest, a leading figure of Latin American liberation theology. Silenced by the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith over 'Church: Charism and Power' (1981), he left the priesthood and Franciscan order in 1992 and later turned to eco-theology, base ecclesial communities and integral ecology.[7] [20] [54]

Clodovis Boff

b. 1944 · Brazil

Clodovis Boff, OSM (b. 1944, Brazil), Brazilian Servite priest and systematic theologian, a co-founder of liberation theology's method who co-wrote 'Introducing Liberation Theology' with his brother Leonardo and authored 'Theology and Praxis'. From 2007 he publicly broke with the current's method, arguing it had put the poor in the place of Christ; he has taught at PUC-Rio and PUC-Paraná.[1] [94] [39]

Jon Sobrino

b. 1938 · El Salvador

Jon Sobrino (b. 1938, Barcelona) is a Spanish-Basque Jesuit who went to El Salvador in the late 1950s and became a leading christologist at the Universidad Centroamericana (UCA). His liberation christology reads Jesus 'from the victims'. Abroad during the 16 November 1989 UCA massacre, he survived and chronicled its martyrs and Óscar Romero; a 2006/07 CDF Notification found 'erroneous or dangerous' propositions in his work.[21] [31] [60] [88] [91]

Ignacio Ellacuría

1930–1989 · El Salvador

Ignacio Ellacuría (1930–1989), Basque-born Jesuit philosopher and theologian in El Salvador and a disciple of Xavier Zubiri. He developed a 'philosophy of historical reality' and the method of 'historicization'. As rector of the UCA (from 1979) he pressed for a negotiated peace; he and seven others were killed by Salvadoran soldiers on 16 November 1989.[75] [62] [31] [57]

Juan Luis Segundo

1925–1996 · Uruguay

Juan Luis Segundo (1925–1996), Uruguayan Jesuit theologian in Montevideo and a founding figure of the current. He developed the 'hermeneutic circle' method centred on ideological suspicion, holding that what liberates theology is its methodology rather than its content, and drew less on Marxist social analysis than some peers.[13] [89]

Hugo Assmann

1933–2008 · Brazil

Hugo Assmann (1933–2008), Brazilian theologian and educator, one of the earliest and most radical methodologists of the current. Exiled during Brazil's military dictatorship, he helped found the Departamento Ecuménico de Investigaciones (DEI) in Costa Rica. He later left the priesthood, married, and turned to critiques of market 'idolatry' and to pedagogy.[85] [73] [77] [1]

Rubem Alves

1933–2014 · Brazil

Brazilian Presbyterian theologian whose 1968 Princeton dissertation coined the phrase 'theology of liberation', making him the current's Protestant pioneer. Persecuted by both the military dictatorship and his own church, he later drifted from systematic theology toward psychoanalysis, chronicles, children's books and a poetic 'theopoetics'.[68] [86] [72] [8]

Frei Betto (Carlos Alberto Libânio Christo)

b. 1944 · Brazil

Brazilian Dominican friar, writer and popular educator (b. 1944, Belo Horizonte). Twice jailed under the military dictatorship (1964; c. 1969–1973), he became a leading organizer of Brazil's comunidades eclesiais de base and their interecclesial meetings. Author of 'Fidel e a Religião' (1985) and later a Fome Zero adviser (2003–04); his links to the Workers' Party and Cuba are documented in the sibling fsp project.[9] [71] [98] [80]

Ernesto Cardenal

1925–2020 · Nicaragua

Ernesto Cardenal (1925–2020), Nicaraguan poet and Catholic priest, a leading voice of the current in Central America. He founded the Solentiname contemplative-artistic community, whose Gospel dialogues became 'El Evangelio en Solentiname'. Sandinista Minister of Culture (1979–1987), suspended from ministry by John Paul II, he later broke with Daniel Ortega and had his suspension lifted by Pope Francis in 2019.[10] [36] [51]

Camilo Torres

1929–1966 · Colombia

Colombian priest and sociologist who co-founded Latin America's first sociology faculty at the Universidad Nacional, led the Frente Unido movement, was laicized in 1965 and joined the ELN guerrilla, dying in his first combat in 1966. A precursor symbol who predates the current, not a liberation theologian: his choice of armed struggle was rejected by its main theologians such as Gustavo Gutiérrez.[11] [1] [97] [63]

Hélder Câmara

1909–1999 · Brazil

Brazilian Catholic prelate, Archbishop of Olinda e Recife (1964–1985) and co-founder of the CNBB (1952). A pastoral 'bishop of the poor' and human-rights advocate under the dictatorship, he is remembered as a precursor and moral inspiration of the current's social-justice strand rather than a systematic theologian; earlier an integralist sympathizer, he later renounced that stance.[1] [79] [81]

Óscar Romero

1917–1980 · El Salvador

Óscar Arnulfo Romero (1917–1980), Archbishop of San Salvador from 1977, the era's most prominent Salvadoran church voice against state repression before his assassination while saying Mass on 24 March 1980. Canonized in 2018 as a martyr killed in odium fidei, not as a liberation theologian; his own relationship to the current is debated among biographers.[12] [93] [65] [67]

Pedro Casaldáliga

1928–2020 · Brazil

Spanish-born Claretian missionary and bishop-prelate of São Félix do Araguaia (1971–2005). A pastoral figure and poet claimed by the current rather than a systematic theologian, he championed Indigenous people and landless peasants amid violent Araguaia land conflicts, co-founded the Comissão Pastoral da Terra, and drew Vatican scrutiny for his activism.[70] [56] [1]

John Paul II

1920–2005 · Vatican

Pope 1978–2005. Warned against political 're-readings' of Christ at Puebla (1979) and presided over the disciplinary era of the 1980s, while his 1986 letter to the Brazilian bishops also used affirming language — both sides cited.[22] [1]

Joseph Ratzinger / Benedict XVI

1927–2022 · Vatican

CDF prefect 1981–2005 (signed Libertatis Nuntius, the Boff Notification, and Libertatis Conscientia), pope 2005–13 (opened Aparecida 2007).[18] [19] [23]

Alfonso López Trujillo

1935–2008 · Colombia

Colombian cardinal (1935–2008). Elected CELAM secretary-general at Sucre, Bolivia, on 22 November 1972 and CELAM president from 1979; widely described as the leading intra-Latin-American institutional opponent of liberation theology. Later president of the Pontifical Council for the Family; died in Rome, 19 April 2008.[101] [105] [1]

Francis (Jorge Bergoglio)

1936–2025 · Argentina / Vatican

Pope 2013–2025. Chaired Aparecida's drafting committee (2007), received Gutiérrez (2013), canonized Romero (2018), restored Cardenal (2019); whether this amounts to a rehabilitation of liberation theology is debated — see disambiguation.[35] [40] [41]

Leo XIV (Robert Prevost)

b. 1955 · USA / Peru / Vatican

Pope from May 2025; spent decades as a missionary and bishop in Peru. Liberation theologians' expressions of hope about his pontificate, and his 'Dilexi te' (2025), are reported with debate about their significance.[42] [24]

Related organizations

CELAM (Consejo Episcopal Latinoamericano e Caribeño)

Founded 1955 · Latin America

The Latin American bishops' council whose general conferences frame the movement's history: Rio 1955, Medellín 1968, Puebla 1979, Santo Domingo 1992, Aparecida 2007, and the First Ecclesial Assembly 2021.[14] [26]

https://www.celam.org/

CEBs (Comunidades Eclesiais de Base)

Grassroots ecclesial base communities, strongest in Brazil, that carried the movement's pastoral practice; estimates of their number at the 1980s peak vary widely between sources and are reported as ranges.[3] [43]

Commentators state that CEB networks supplied cadres to Brazil's Workers' Party in the 1980s — an attributed, contested characterization.

Cristianos por el Socialismo (Christians for Socialism)

Founded 1971 · Chile

Movement begun by Chilean priests supporting socialism (April 1971), with its first Latin American Encounter in Santiago in April 1972; banned for clergy by the Chilean bishops after the 1973 coup.[29] [16]

ISAL (Iglesia y Sociedad en América Latina)

Founded 1961 · Latin America

The Protestant 'church and society' network (journal 'Cristianismo y Sociedad') that formed a parallel precursor stream, with bridge figures such as Rubem Alves and Richard Shaull.[28]

Derived and parallel liberation theologies

Black theology (James Cone), feminist and womanist theologies, indigenous 'teología india', Dalit and Minjung theologies, and later ecological developments — related currents that dialogue with, but are distinct from, the Latin American movement.[1]

Disambiguation & nuance

Liberation Theology is politically contested terrain. The entries below record nuances that are easy to flatten; each characterization is attributed to its source.

A current, not an organization

Liberation theology has no founding charter, headquarters or membership roster. It is a family of theologies; chronology entries reference specific books, conferences and persons — never 'membership'.[1] [4]

1984 ≠ blanket condemnation

Libertatis Nuntius (1984) criticizes 'certain aspects' — specifically the uncritical borrowing of Marxist analysis — and explicitly does not condemn liberation from poverty as a theme; Libertatis Conscientia (1986) develops a positive theology of liberation. Gutiérrez himself maintained that 'the Vatican never condemned the theology of liberation' — cited as his claim.[18] [19] [38]

Different figures, different outcomes

Boff received a Notification and a silencing (1985); Sobrino a Notification without silencing (2006/07); Gutiérrez was scrutinized but never sanctioned. 'The Vatican condemned liberation theology' collapses three distinct outcomes.[20] [21] [38]

Francis's stance is debated

Rehabilitative readings cite Aparecida, the 2013 Gutiérrez meeting, Romero's canonization and Cardenal's restoration; skeptical readings cite Bergoglio's formation in the distinct Argentine 'teología del pueblo' and analysts who say he distanced himself from figures like Boff. Both readings are attributed, not asserted.[40] [41] [35]

Romero and Camilo Torres caveats

Romero was canonized as a martyr, not 'as a liberation theologian', and his relationship to the movement is debated; Camilo Torres predates the movement and chose armed struggle, which its main theologians did not advocate.[12] [11]

The PT / Foro de São Paulo link is an attributed claim

Claims that CEB networks fed Brazil's Workers' Party and that Frei Betto brokered the PT–Cuba relationship around the Foro de São Paulo's 1990 founding appear chiefly in critical literature and biographies. The sibling cronologia/fsp project documents this connection with the same attribute-don't-assert discipline.[43] [9]
